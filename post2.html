Dreaming in Black & White: American Film Noir
Catherine Alderete

History and Visual Style of Noir

Written by Catherine Alderete and Nicholas Geoghegan

Proofread and Edited by Nicholas Geoghegan

“A passion for the past and present, but also a fear of the future.” –“Notes on Film Noir” by Paul Schrader

American film noir is renowned for its convoluted plotlines, but it is equally characterized by a distinct set of moods, including paranoia, claustrophobia, death, and crime, which are effectively conveyed through its visual style. Rather than being primarily defined by its narrative structure, noir stands out among other genres due to its mood and tone, as exemplified by iconic films like The Maltese Falcon, Double Indemnity, Murder, My Sweet and Laura. The origins of these stories can be traced back to the early 1930s, where American writers such as Dashiell Hammett, Raymond Chandler, and James M. Cain created the foundation of hard-boiled detective stories, serving as the inspiration for their cinematic adaptations (Naremore, 1998, p. 3).

Moreover, surrealism has had a significant impact on the genre as a whole. It has always played a crucial role in the reception and interpretation of any artwork associated with the label "noir." The genre frequently employs adjectives such as oneiric, strange, and cruel, which resonate with the language of surrealists. These descriptors serve to unveil the hidden depths of the narrative and imbue the whodunit mystery with an amplified sense of magnitude (Naremore, 1998, p. 17-22).

The expansion of surrealism in the United States owes much to influential contemporary artists like Salvador Dali, Andre Breton, Max Ernst, and Marcel Duchamp. Additionally, the German expressionists, including Robert Sidomak, Curtis Bernhardt, and Fritz Lang, played a vital role in shaping the genre with their distinctive high-contrast photography and atmospheric determinism, which added a touch of impending doom. Through their work, they exposed the darker underbelly of the American character (Paul Schrader, 1972, p. 32).

Often considered the genesis of film noir, The Maltese Falcon established the genre as we understand it today. This film elevated Humphrey Bogart’s career, defining him in pen and paper as the original hard-boiled detective. In his debut picture, director John Huston effortlessly (and perhaps unknowingly) defined cinema’s perspective on the detective crime story. The film is filled with merciless fistfights, smoky interior sets, high-contrast shadows and venetian blinds, coolheaded crime bosses, fruitless love affairs, and so much more. Alongside the poster, you can see daily progress reports and production memos that would be sent to Warner Bros. executives and the film’s producers.

Double Indemnity is a timeless love story that was destined to end in tragedy. This film was one of the first major noirs to employ a non-linear narrative, as our protagonist narrates the events of the film in retrospect, adding an element of suspense and moral introspection. The chiaroscuro lighting and expressionistic visuals heightened the sense of tension and unease, contributing to the film's noir ambiance. Billy Wilder’s immaculate script is full of quintessential film noir quips, one-liners, and no-nonsense discourse. Owing to Universal Pictures’ strict archival policies, production materials from this film remain unavailable for public viewing.

Mildred Pierce was brought to life by Joan Crawford, whose central character defied traditional gender roles and became entangled in a web of deceit and manipulation. While she doesn't fit the archetypal femme fatale role seen in many film noirs, her actions and motivations are complex and morally ambiguous, reflecting the noir sensibility. The use of shadows and low-key lighting heightened the atmosphere of tension and uncertainty, with some of the most exaggerated shadows in all of film noir filling every frame of this beautifully shot film.
